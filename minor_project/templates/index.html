About Project

Welcome to our Cluster Comet! Our platform is dedicated to exploring and understanding the intricate networks that shape communities. Through the implementation of various algorithms such as Louvain's algorithm, Girvan-Newman algorithm, Modularity Optimization algorithm, and Label Propagation algorithm, we aim to unravel the hidden structures within these networks.

Community detection plays a pivotal role in understanding how individuals or entities interact and form groups or clusters within a network. Our platform provides a user-friendly interface to apply these algorithms, allowing users to visualize, analyze, and interpret the communities present in their networks.

Louvain's algorithm and Girvan-Newman algorithm are renowned for their effectiveness in detecting communities by optimizing network modularity. Meanwhile, the Modularity Optimization algorithm refines this process by iteratively improving the network's modularity. On the other hand, Label Propagation algorithm utilizes label information to identify communities based on node similarities.

Whether you're a researcher, student, or enthusiast keen on understanding the dynamics of communities within networks, our platform offers a powerful toolkit to explore and analyze these structures efficiently.

Join us in this journey of discovery and exploration as we delve into the fascinating world of community detection!

How it works

Make sure your CSV file has only 2 columns with the column heading names as “source” and “target” as Community Detection works on only 2 column dataset.

1. Upload CSV file.

2. Select the type of network.

3. Select from the algorithms list.

4. Go for Execute.

5. The output will be visible in a new webpage.

Popular Case Study

Zachary Karate Club

In the '70s, Wayne W. Zachary uncovered a social marvel within the Zachary Karate Club at a university. This 34-member club, led by "Mr. Hi," became Zachary's exploration ground for social networks.

A rift split the club, dividing members between loyalty to Mr. Hi and a new faction. Zachary meticulously documented these ties, creating a network graph that symbolized connections—subtle interactions and shared bonds among individuals.

Beyond martial arts, this club became a sociological treasure trove. It offered insights into social networks, showcasing the impact of division and the intricate ties that shape us.

In the web app's world of community detection algorithms, the Zachary Karate Club remains a pivotal case study. It sheds light on the complexities of human connections and the algorithms' prowess in deciphering these networks.

Louvains Algorithm

Louvain's algorithm's success in generating four distinct communities within the Zachary Karate Club dataset highlights its effectiveness in detecting intricate social structures.

The algorithm optimizes modularity, efficiently dividing the club into cohesive groups. It emphasizes strong intra-community connections and weaker inter-community ties, revealing the network's internal structures with clarity.

Notably, its computational efficiency ensured swift analysis, identifying these four communities promptly. Louvain's adaptability to diverse community sizes allowed it to capture both cohesive clusters and subtle substructures within the club's network.

By precisely identifying these communities, Louvain's algorithm validated the evident divisions within the club while showcasing its prowess in capturing detailed social dynamics. Its ability to discern these nuanced structures underscores its value in understanding complex social networks, offering insights into the Zachary Karate Club's intricate social fabric.

Girvan Newman Algorithm

Girvan Newman's algorithm, applied to the Zachary Karate Club dataset, diverged from expectations by consolidating the network into two communities instead of the anticipated four distinct clusters.

This algorithm identifies communities by progressively removing central edges, aiming to break the network into smaller clusters. In this case, it unexpectedly fused the club's members into two larger, more interconnected groups.

The emphasis on edge centrality likely pinpointed crucial connections holding the club together, leading to this binary split. What was thought to be four separate clusters appeared to merge into two more cohesive communities under this algorithm.

Despite the unexpected result, Girvan Newman's approach shed light on pivotal connections' influence on community formation within the club's social network. This consolidation into two communities offered a unique perspective on the network's core connections and their impact on shaping the club's social dynamics, providing a nuanced understanding of the Zachary Karate Club's structural composition.

Modularity Optimisation Algorithm

The Modularity Optimization Algorithm, applied to the Zachary Karate Club dataset, diverged from expectations by revealing three distinct communities instead of the anticipated four clusters.

This algorithm aims to maximize internal connectivity within communities while minimizing connections between them. In parsing the club's network, it surprisingly organized members into three separate yet interconnected groups, showcasing cohesive social clusters.

Despite the initial anticipation of four communities, this algorithm's focus on strengthening internal bonds and reducing inter-community links unveiled a nuanced division. It delineated three communities with strong internal ties but some connections between them.

Though unexpected, this division into three communities offered valuable insights into the club's social structure. The algorithm's approach highlighted the subtle social affiliations within the network, offering a deeper understanding of the Zachary Karate Club's community dynamics and the varying degrees of cohesion among its members.

Label Propagation Algorithm

When the Label Propagation Algorithm was applied to the Zachary Karate Club dataset, it revealed an intriguing breakdown of the network into three distinct communities, differing from the anticipated four clusters.

This algorithm operates by assigning initial labels to nodes and propagating them through network connections, where nodes adopt the most frequent label of their neighbors. In this scenario, it segmented the club's members into three separate but interconnected communities, each representing cohesive social clusters.

Despite the expectation of four communities, this algorithm's approach led to the emergence of three distinct groups, characterized by nodes adopting predominant labels within their connected clusters.

This unexpected division into three communities provided an insightful view of the club's social structure. The Label Propagation Algorithm's method illuminated the organic social affiliations within the network, offering a nuanced understanding of the Zachary Karate Club's community dynamics and highlighting the influential clusters and their interconnections among club members.

Conclusion

Louvain's algorithm successfully generated four well-defined communities, showcasing clear and cohesive clusters. Girvan Newman's approach surprisingly condensed the network into two communities, revealing pivotal connections and a more unified structure than anticipated.

Modularity Optimization and Label Propagation algorithms both arrived at three communities, emphasizing strong internal connections while showing varying degrees of inter-community links.

In summary, Louvain's method provided clear divisions, Girvan Newman's highlighted pivotal connections, and both Modularity Optimization and Label Propagation showcased nuanced community structures. Each algorithm contributed unique insights, offering a multifaceted understanding of the Zachary Karate Club's social dynamics, portraying the complexities inherent in community detection within networks.

Our Case Study

Highschool

In the labyrinth of a high school's social network dataset, nodes encapsulate students, while edges signify friendships. Algorithms like Louvain or Modularity Optimisation dissect this digital mesh, uncovering clusters that represent potential social communities.

These clusters are snapshots of interconnected relationships. Some might resonate with sports camaraderie, while others might gleam with shared artistic passions or academic pursuits.

As the algorithms decode the network, they unveil these cohesive communities, each pulsating with shared connections and potential shared interests. The analysis refrains from drawing definitive conclusions about the exact nature of these clusters, as the complexity of human relationships extends beyond mere digital representations.

This dataset offers a peek into the intricate social dynamics of high school life, revealing the interconnectedness fostered by friendships and shared affinities. Algorithms strive to unveil the hidden structures within this network, providing insights into the potential formations of social communities among the students.

Louvains Algorithm

The Louvain algorithm dissected the high school social network dataset, analyzing students' friendships encoded as edges between nodes. Its strategy centered on optimizing modularity, seeking densely connected groups while minimizing connections between clusters.

By scrutinizing these connections iteratively, Louvain uncovered five distinct communities within the network. These clusters highlighted groups of students tightly linked by friendships or shared interests, offering a glimpse into the school's social fabric.

Louvain's approach refrained from defining the specifics of these communities, encouraging users to explore and interpret the nuances of each cluster. This method provided a valuable insight into how friendships formed cohesive groups within the high school. It empowered users to delve deeper into the dynamics and intricacies of these detected communities, shedding light on the social dynamics within the student body.

Girvan Newman Algorithm

Girvan-Newman algorithm took a unique path through the high school network, methodically isolating crucial connections within the friendships dataset. By progressively removing key edges, it unveiled a network divided into two distinct communities.

In contrast to Louvain's multiple clusters, Girvan-Newman highlighted a clear polarization within the social landscape, emphasizing a pronounced division between two major groups. This approach provided a contrasting perspective, spotlighting either strong social divisions or tightly bonded alliances between these two significant communities.

The algorithm's outcome offers users a different lens to explore the network's dynamics, showcasing the broader segregation or cohesion within the student body. Understanding the nuanced differences between Girvan-Newman and Louvain's interpretations provides a comprehensive view of the diverse structures and potential social divisions shaping the high school's social network.

Modularity Optimisation Algorithm

The modularity optimization algorithm navigated the high school social network, meticulously examining friendships represented by node connections. It aimed to identify cohesive communities while maximizing internal connections and minimizing connections between clusters.

In its exploration, modularity optimization uncovered a network divided into four distinct communities. These clusters highlighted groups of students closely linked by friendships and shared interests, providing a nuanced view of the school's social fabric.

Unlike Girvan-Newman's binary division or Louvain's multiple clusters, modularity optimization struck a balance, revealing four communities that showcased both cohesion within smaller groups and distinguishable boundaries between them. This approach offered a middle ground, capturing a more granular view of the social dynamics within the student body.

The algorithm's outcome invites users to delve into these four communities, understanding the interplay of friendships and shared affiliations that define each cluster. Exploring these nuances enriches the understanding of the diverse structures shaping the high school's social network, portraying a multifaceted landscape of connections and interactions among its students.

Label Propagation Algorithm

The label propagation algorithm navigated the high school social network, aiming to identify communities by spreading labels across interconnected nodes. It revealed six distinct clusters, each representing cohesive groups formed by shared connections and friendships.

Diverging from previous methods, label propagation adopted a decentralized approach, allowing nodes to adapt labels based on immediate connections. This method showcased a more intricate segmentation of the network, emphasizing the adaptable nature of social relationships among students.

By uncovering six diverse communities, this algorithm provided a nuanced view of the school's social fabric. Its decentralized label adoption mirrored the fluidity of friendships, capturing a rich tapestry of social clusters within the student body.

Exploring these clusters reveals the dynamic nature of social affiliations and interactions. The algorithm's decentralized approach offers insights into the adaptable nature of social groupings, enriching the understanding of the complex social dynamics shaping the high school network.

Conclusion

The diverse outcomes from the four algorithms—Louvain, Girvan-Newman, modularity optimization, and label propagation—illuminate the multifaceted nature of the high school social network. Louvain's multiple clusters showcased varied but interconnected communities, Girvan-Newman's binary division emphasized clear polarization, modularity optimization struck a balance with cohesive yet distinguishable communities, and label propagation revealed a fluid, decentralized network with several nuanced clusters.

These distinct perspectives underscore the complexity of social dynamics among high school students. While Louvain and modularity optimization highlighted cohesive groups with varying degrees of connectivity, Girvan-Newman's binary outcome hinted at potential strong divisions or alliances. In contrast, label propagation portrayed a fluid, adaptable network reflecting the ever-changing nature of social relationships.

The amalgamation of these outcomes paints a rich tapestry of the high school's social fabric, illustrating the intricate interplay of friendships, shared interests, and social circles. Understanding these diverse interpretations offers a comprehensive view of the network's structures, dynamics, and potential divisions, emphasizing the need for nuanced analyses to capture the nuanced nature of adolescent social interactions.
